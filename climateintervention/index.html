Overview

We need your expertise and perspective to identify and address the critical research questions that will expand our understanding of the ethical and social dynamics that need to be considered as the world debates deploying large-scale CDR (Carbon Dioxide Removal, with marine, mCDR, considered in a separate event from other types of CDR) and SRM (Solar Radiation Management) strategies. The NSF-sponsored workshops are designed to frame the bounds of viable strategies for CDR and SRM while addressing the pressing challenges of the ethical and social dynamics that will be critical in shaping the development and implementation of these strategies.

From TK- TK in October 2024, the U.S. National Science Foundation is virtually gathering a group of selected participants to discuss one of society’s most pressing issues: climate intervention.

Participants will consider the ethical and social dynamics arising as the world debates deploying large-scale Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR), both from the sea and land, and Solar Radiation Management (SRM) strategies.

During a series of workshops, a set of experts will propose its visions for what is scientifically possible. Another set will identify gaps through a social science lens. Everyone will discuss research opportunities to fill those gaps.

The workshops welcome outside-the-box thinking to help frame the bounds of viable strategies for CDR and SRM while addressing the pressing challenges of the ethical and social dynamics that will be so critical in developing and implementing these strategies.

Although these actions, collectively referred to as climate intervention, encompass a broad and growing range of techniques, they each come with potential impacts and unintended consequences to both the environment and society.

The Challenge

As we face the urgent need to mitigate climate change, we must weigh the risks of implementing CDR and SRM strategies against the risks of inaction. This requires a holistic and iterative approach, integrating advances in both physical and social sciences. The ethical and social dynamics are central, as they have prevented broader acceptance of these strategies to date. Furthermore, considering the engineering feasibility, scales, and public health implications of these interventions is crucial to their potential successful implementation and public acceptance.

The Event

We are planning a series of five half-day workshops, complemented by an initial microlab session. The first three workshops will focus on the ocean and mCDR, the atmosphere and SRM, and the land and CDR and include both physical and social scientists. The final two workshops will be limited to social scientists, with key physical scientists also attending to provide necessary insights.
